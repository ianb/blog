Instead of Privacy

When I talk about the idea behind Hotdish (as I have here in Defaulting to Together, Toward a Next Level Of Collaboration and …) people will frequently get weirded out by the privacy implications.

I want to understand whatever people feel as what they feel. But of course this reaction isn’t what people feel, this is what they are worried they will feel. Or if they are more direct, what worries them about what I am describing. The tool itself is as yet unknown.

The things I’ve described, and the things I’ve been building, are not technologically that advanced. There’s some cool hacks, but most of the hacks are very normal hacks. (Though whatever the level, there’s admittedly a lot of hacks.) And yet I keep feeling like I’m doing something very new — I search around for prior art, and find very little. Why is this new? Is there some fundamental problem with the idea that I’m missing? Something that is destined to sink this idea?

One explanation is that there is something that has many times sunk these kinds of ideas at their time of conception: privacy. (And when it comes to privacy concerns sinking ideas, I am in the belly of the beast.) And I would fall into this trap often, constantly worrying about privacy, trying to massage this idea so it didn’t fall into a fear trap, instead of trying to work towards the greatest positive value. (This explanation is by no means sufficient, but I think it may be a part.)

If I go into the office without pants I will expect to be embarassed. This is no empty concern — the fear apparently weighs on me enough to show up in my anxiety dreams. If I am at home I trust I can go without pants without any repercussions. I would be uncomfortable if instead of me using a remote presence robot at the office, my coworkers had a remote presence robot installed in my home.

Our personal computing right now doesn’t have an office mode, a public mode, it only has the private-at-home mode. We can call out, put things in the post, but we can’t just be in public, there’s nothing fully inclusive, nothing where we are set to automatically opt-in to working together.

That’s not quite true: personal computing at work may not be entirely private. But the lack of privacy on a work computer is worse than the remote presence robot at home, instead it’s a secret camera. The secret camera of the HTTP proxy, or the keyboard logger. A good office isn’t a panopticon, but it’s also not a gathering of hermits. It’s public, and transparently public.
