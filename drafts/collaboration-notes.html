Collaboration Notes

So What Does This Look Like?

So far I’ve written about abstract qualities of the experience. What would it actually look like?

Privacy Through Notification

Minimal interfaces are cool. Especially when you are working with something that adds to a page. But we’re talking about potentially pretty significant privacy leaks here, even security problems depending on how much the collaborator can do.

The primary way I think we can address this is by making sure the collaboration interface is clear, ubiquitous, and does not fade into the background. Whenever you are doing something, it should be clear to you that you are doing it in front of other people.

I also think opting in to new domains as part of a collaboration group would be useful. Most sites either are, or are not, intended for a specific group. In my own work *.mozilla.org is fine, as is github.com and github.io. reddit.com probably isn’t; there might be exceptions, so the “include once” and “include forever” distinction makes sense.

People are definitely going to make mistakes, but such is life.

Another Way To Think About Tabs

One of the first inspirations for thinking about collaborative browsing was the largely vaporous suggestion that collaboration should be part of Firefox Tab Groups/Panorama. Whatever it is, I think some more sophisticated way to deal with a set of pages is necessary. Now we’d be talking about our own pages, and other people’s pages. And people may want to refer to pages and pieces of pages. That may be the underlying metaphor behind Mode Changes: go to a new tab, fork a tab, review someone else’s tabs, etc. The linear list of tabs along the top has no room for anything so sophisticated.

And we’re not just talking about tabs, we’re talking about pages in a more sophisticated way. Tabs are perhaps even a distraction, a particular bit of UI we’ve become familiar with. We might want to watch someone else’s page action, “fork” their page (which could be as simple as loading a URL locally), tell someone else we’d like them to view our page, including exact scroll position and selection, or give someone a record of what we’ve done.

One thing the idea of “tabs” does give us is a two-level grouping: we have both OS-level windows, and for each window a list of tabs. I fear this distinction is unclear to many people. If it wasn’t, then we could imaging all collaboration groups existed in distinct windows (or on mobile devices, perhaps individual apps), and then we have a further breakdown of pages and tabs and other people’s tabs from there. Using Pinned Tabs we might allow a bit of ubiquitous non-collaborative content across windows (Pin Harder!) Or maybe we have another super-organizer on top of tabs but below windows. Tab-tabs.

It’s an aside, but over the Mozilla Summit there was a discussion about what Firefox can do to be better for web developers. The biggest takeaway wasn’t the developer tools, it was tab management — nothing specific to developers, just to users who use the web a lot and want the most out of their tool.

The Collaboration Tools

So what do we actually show people?

Seeing each other’s mouse pointers is very compelling — it’s what people notice first, and seems very attractive. I think this is more than just some sort of raw broad appeal. The mouse cursor is like the body language of web interaction. If the cursor is moving around, the person is very engaged with the page — they might not click on anything or do anything, but you see that movement. The cursor often also indicates intentions, people move cursors to where they are thinking about doing something.

Text selection is another tool, more precise in many cases than a cursor.

Form focus and keyboard activity are also good to show. We show them separately in TogetherJS, but probably they should be combined. Form focus can often be unintentional. We don’t care if a field gets autofocused, we only care if the focus represents the user’s intention to interact with that element.

Being able to show a snippet of a page is something I’ve found myself frequently wanting (something TogetherJS does not yet do). This is somewhere between “go here now!” and “maybe look over here, m’kay?” — I really want someone to see some part of a page. This might take the form of a screenshot that links to the original page.

When using screensharing there’s potential to rewind an interaction. This could answer the “huh, how did we get here?” question. You can’t rewind normal apps, this is an example of someplace where screensharing (unlike simple peer-to-peer collaboration) enables some really neat features.

When in screensharing/demonstration mode you may want to “fork” the page. This would open the page as normal, and then do what state initialization is possible in peer-to-peer mode — changing form field values, scrolling to the proper position, and doing any custom synchronization the application has defined.

Feasibility

Obviously making something cool is cool. Fantasy tools work great! Can we actually make this?

Browser Mirror and TogetherJS together represent two distinct and fairly in-depth experiments. I think a full tool needs to include both approaches. We have not entirely demonstrated that these techniques can be robust on the wider web, or at least that when they fail they do so in a way that’s reasonably transparent and at least partially usable. But I feel quite optimistic on this count.

For ambient awareness we may need some new UI metaphors and organizational tools. Doing this well is non-trivial, and it must transparent, understandable, but also not too intrusive. And it may involve new ways to organize browsing contexts, which means mucking about in fairly contentious parts of the browser.

Because this necessarily means multiple participants, we have to worry about version mismatches. For screensharing scenarios we can to some degree rely on the source browser to bootstrap the viewing browser, thus absolutely ensuring consistent versions of client code. But for many other parts of a system we run a bigger risk of conflicting or out of date clients.

With a UI and group interactions that need refinement and perhaps specialization for different kinds of groups, it may be infeasible to come up with a UI that is widely optimal. The challenge here: can at least part of the UI be distributed and developed separately from the browser? If so, version mismatch might also be mitigated.

Collaborative tools necessarily work in heterogeneous environments. If we demand Firefox (and to begin with, specifically Firefox Nightly) how many groups could we reasonably support? Far too few, I think – requiring everyone in a group to switch to a single browser just to preview these capabilities is too unrealistic. If we rely only on current cross-browser capabilities then we can’t do much beyond what TogetherJS already does. Any attempt to standardize would be folly.

In some cases cross-browser isn’t hard at all. For the viewer in a screenshare environment you might almost have to work to keep it from being cross-browser. And viewing alone is useful functionality! We don’t need parity across all browsers, we just need a functional and useful experience. Extensions may also be a partial solution, though they could also require excessive effort for what may still be an inferior experience. Also I don’t believe newer versions of Internet Explorer offer any extension mechanism.

Security looms as a risk in all of this.

Intermediate Products

I am distrustful of a final vision of this suite of tools. What is really feasible to implement? What is useful? What is attractive? What is error-prone, or dangerous?

What usable tools can we produce along the way?

One is simply Browser-Mirror-style screen sharing, with no particular persistent sessions, view-only or almost-view-only, and explicit link-based invitations.

Another component is chat (text, audio, video) in the browser context. This is actually already happening under the name Talkilla. In another sense this has also happened as part of the Social API, allowing content to be more persistent in the browser, upon which you could build chat.

TogetherJS of course is another tool. It live entirely in content, but it can incrementally lean on the browser to implement pieces. This would solve some current usability issues, like needing to reconnect audio chat whenever you navigate.

We could provide communication channels that content could take advantage of, while managing the communications through our in-browser presence system.

We could expose page information to the Social API, allowing collaboration tools to be built in this sidebar.

We could explore peer-to-peer authentication, for managing the identity of your peers. Right now most authentication schemes use some server authority.

We could provide basic browsing information (URLs, and activity/attention) for ambient awareness.
