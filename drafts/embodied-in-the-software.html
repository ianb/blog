Embodied In The Software

“Embodied Cognition” generally refers to integrating physical senses and action into the AI. But instead of preconceptions of what embodiment might be, I have been thinking about how the AI can embody whatever it is. (“Interoception”)

More specifically: how can the AI embody its software, application, or environment?

Inputs

For instance I’ve been developing a voice-based chat interface. The voice is transcribed into text for a variety of reasons1, so the AI cannot interpret the audio, its senses are in this respect dulled. And yet I can at least give it the context that is available:

<speechTranscript>
[insert transcript]
</speechTranscript>

That alone tells the AI how the text was collected and what it should expect. And it works… the AI will interpret the text more generously, assuming that it may be mistranscribed. I haven’t tried providing a confidence score or alternate word transcriptions, but I should.

There are lots of inputs that can help explain the context of the application, but won’t be included in the AI’s context unless you go to some effort:

  1. The local time of day
  2. The timespan between messages
  3. Any diversions the user took between messages
  4. The device being used
  5. The screen the user is looking at, and any information on the screen
  6. The provenance of the information it is provided (such as the transcript having the provenance of recorded speech)
  7. For information provided to the AI: the date at which the information was collected or verified
  8. Knowledge about context or history that has been trimmed because of space concerns

This information can keep expanding, but it shouldn’t be provided literally at each step. For instance for 2 I only make note of timespans over 10 minutes, and specify timespans instead of timestamps, allowing the AI to see those notable pauses without having to calculate time differences or scan each message. You must treat the AI as another user and apply a kind of user interface design, finding out how to best present the information and controls.

Capabilities

This is easier because we have a widely used way to give the AI capabilities: Tools. A Tool is simply a definition of a structured command the AI can output alongside its textual response. For instance if there is a setAlarm() tool then the AI can assume it is capable of setting an alarm.

There’s still a few things frequently missing:

  1. There may be other actions that aren’t represented as tools.
  2. It’s seldom specified that the tools are comprehensive and no other actions are possible.
  3. Even with both these, the AI will need more documentation when it consistently guesses incorrectly at some boundaries of its abilities.

Cognitive limits

The AI has been trained on enough truncated transcripts that it has adapted to filling in missing past history and expecting that some old history will be lost.

If the software is doing more to modify and curate the prompt then that may also be worth specifying. For instance in my application I send photos the user uploads on the first request, but leave the photos out of the history. I should be telling the AI that it has one chance to look at the photos, and if it needs to remember any details it should make note of those details.

Things you might want to specify:

  1. Can the AI think or act without the user initiating an interaction?
  2. How much of its history is missing? (How many conversation turns or how much time.)
  3. If it responds with some information: an estimate of how long it will have access to that information before it is culled from history
  4. Will it have another chance to respond after invoking a Tool?
  5. If you give it thinking scratchpads2, will it see that scratchpad in the next request?

  1. It is possible to send the raw audio to an LLM and have it respond directly, giving it more ability to respond to tone and pauses, as well as adjust its transcript based on the context. But this is more expensive, harder to debug, and less flexible in certain cases. ↩

  2. a scratchpad is a place where you let the AI write down a thought process before creating the “real” output. Reasoning models typically do this automatically, but any model can be asked to follow this step. ↩
