Resilience, capitalism, markets, and global warming

First, I must start with this:

Without me, the world would be almost exactly as it would be with me.

If I was replaced with another person, or if I was erased from the earth entirely, the world would not change.

The world is different for my family, for my children, for my children’s only imagined children, because I exist. But for you, reader – even if you know me well, even if I am your friend – for you, the world would be the same without me.

The world at large, the march of history, in the face of this I am nothing.

Of course what is true for me is also true for you. But it is important that I state this as narrator. I speak as a nothing in the flow of history.

As is my predilection, I was insistent with my drunken rants tonight. It was not unprovoked, but it was built on an internal monologiue unleashed, as my drunken rants so often are.

What is important

Do we really care about global warming?

The challenge of virtue signalling.

The humility of true dedication: to do what is right, despite what feels right.

The self-consistency of capitalism

Money creates capital, capital controls money. This creates a self-reinforcing system that is powerful. It is painfully incomplete, but its self-consistent power dominates even its incredible incompleteness.

The resilience of markets

Markets serve their purpose, of information transfer, of priority setting, of optimization

Markets can work against you

The straw conundrum. Replace plastic straws and you get something worse than plastic straws.

Markets can include moralism

The case of safety: not naturally a market good, but with regulation (and tort law) markets include safety factors.

Markets could include whatever we want them to.

Markets will defeat indirect moralism

Back to the straw conundrum. If we don’t engage the market, it will undo us.

Markets aren’t everything: much of our world is beyond markets

Where I talk about regulation, large segments like energy. Places where tradeoffs can be thought about at a big scale.

Abundance can destroy even markets

Maybe a future where none of this comes true can still be good.
