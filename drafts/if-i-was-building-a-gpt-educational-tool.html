If I was building a GPT educational tool

There’s lots of talk about ChatGPT and education. Lots of that talk is worrying about cheating, which I understand, but isn’t very fun. Much more fun is to think:

How can Large Language Models like GPT be positively applied to education?

If I was going to tackle that (and yes, I’d like that opportunity, but no, I have no specific plans) these are some of the thoughts about how I’d want to go about it:

  1. The primary focus should start with pedagogy and it should stay with pedagogy.
    1. Not business cases, not assessment, not plagiarism detection, not portfolios, not abuse.
    2. Some of these things will need to be addressed in the service of a focus on pedagogy.
  2. Think seriously about the medium of communication:
    1. You can type at ChatGPT and it will respond. This is a very reasonable starting point.
    2. The tool should be available and accessible at the moment that it’s needed. I’m not sure what that moment is (another point of investigation), but typing into chat may not be the right thing at that moment.
    3. Younger children are slow typers and may not be literate enough to make good use of it. Voice is an obvious alternative. (But maybe not the only alternative.)
    4. Voice is pretty awkward to use in the classroom. But it’s worth investigating.
    5. Output doesn’t just have to be text. ChatGPT has Markdown output, but I believe more is possible, possibly with complementary systems or compound interactions that create richer output.
      1. As an example if you ask ChatGPT to solve an algebra problem step by step, it will do so. You could change its narrative response into something animated.
    6. ChatGPT is pretty good at multiple choice. It can offer next steps while also allowing you to choose something off the list.
  3. I’m not very worried about accuracy:
    1. Creating domains of instruction makes it clearer how to keep it informed.
    2. With things like math problems it often gets the approach correct but the arithmetic wrong. Let kids be critical. Don’t make it an assessment tool! You can take “answers” out entirely when that’s appropriate and only let it discuss approaches.
    3. Building this stuff takes time, and GPT (or other models) will get better while that’s happening.
  4. Students need structure and framing:
    1. It’s okay to just jam with ChatGPT and find stuff out. That’s how everyone uses it today, it’s driven by curiosity or purpose and when those are exhausted then you take a break. This should be available.
    2. But that’s not enough for a classroom setting or for educational purposes. It would be cool if every student was naturally self-motivated and driven toward goals, but that’s not a real thing. The students aren’t flawed just because they aren’t self-directed.
    3. ChatGPT should be an ally of the teacher and the curriculum. Teachers should be able to share their plans and goals with ChatGPT and have it make use of that.
      1. The same techniques ChatGPT uses to avoid offering opinions, brainstorm bullying, or do other things it can’t or shouldn’t do, can be applied to classroom direction.
      2. Without classroom management you don’t get to teach. This is also true for classroom tools.
  5. Education happens best in a community:
    1. The tool should be naturally social, with the ability to share in the classroom community.
    2. It may be better to err on the side of transparency in the classroom. This mitigates some abuse, and feels like a good default.
    3. Sharing excitement is great, and being excited about the classroom work helps motivate keeping on task.
  6. The tool should be contextually aware and responsive:
    1. This might be an opportunity to think about those same ideas about communication: some of the communication isn’t asking a question, but being present to watch other work that’s going on.
    2. Like spell check or grammar checking, it shouldn’t go in and fix things directly. It can give very direct feedback and exact changes, but by making the student apply those changes it keeps them in the driver’s seat and aware of the changes.
      1. But a Socratic approach might also be appropriate, and seems feasible: have it phrase its critique as questions.
      2. And the student should be empowered to ignore these critiques: the AI is not in charge! The AI’s lack of ego is a feature.
    3. This is all most accessible on a computer. I don’t like the idea of forcing all work onto a computer. OCR of kids’ handwriting is a non-starter. Maybe having them read out their work to the computer is reasonable in some cases.
  7. I’m concerned about isolation…
    1. Computers are naturally isolated. A class full of students using computers doesn’t have the energy and aliveness of other activities.
    2. Maybe it shouldn’t be a personal-computer-mediated tool. Alexa is an example of a communal tool. It’s exact interaction style isn’t right for a class, but it’s not hopeless to imagine other form factors.
    3. Mostly I don’t know, but it’s worth continuing to think about.
  8. There will likely be multiple entities:
    1. If ChatGPT is an entity but not an assessment tool… there still will be assessment tools.
    2. Things like visualizations are best seen as complementary tools.
    3. GPT and identity is… weird. A singular identity is something you create, not a built-in aspect of it.

This is all very outcome-focused. This may be getting ahead, there’s also a question of process:
