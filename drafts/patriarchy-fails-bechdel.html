“The Patriarchy” fails the Bechdel test

I really like the Bechdel test (named after the author of a comic). First, it’s simple:

Does the work feature at least two women who talk to each other about something other than a man?

It’s not a high bar, but a surprising amount of media fails the test.

Second: it focuses on the content. It’s not about how the media is made, just about what it is.

I like the test because there’s a hidden depth and subtlety to it. A movie that fails the Bechdel Test may have a strong female character. But the Bechdel Test doesn’t just ask for a strong woman, it doesn’t even demand anything specific of a female character. Instead it asks: does this present a world in which women live lives? When the camera shifts away do their characters disappear in wisps of smoke, only to be recreated when they are needed again? Are women satellites of men, or plot devices?

If they are people, if their characters persist beyond how they are needed by men, we should expect they interact, that these female characters need each other and not only men, that they want and consider things besides men.

“The Patriarchy” is of course a common political call-out, the all-being source of all that is society.

The Patriarchy presents a vision of society in which women receive society, while men make society.

Women have their place in The Patriarchy, but it’s as an individual. Society is put upon women. They can resist it. They can redefine it for themselves. But they are forever tourists. It’s a model where women do not have responsibility for society.

For instance, The Patriarchy is frequently blamed for the dominance and strictness of gender roles.

“The Patriarchy” is also painfully vague. What is it? Who is it? It is presented as an indivisible thing. It is presented as a thing.

When something matters, how it is matters.
