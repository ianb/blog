Thursday, September 12nd, 2019

Joining Firefox Voice

I have recently joined a new team and group at Mozilla, in Emerging Technologies, Voice Interfaces, and specifically the project creating a browser-based voice assistant.

Being back in the world of prototyping new products and user experiences is comfortable and enjoyable. It reaffirms my own understanding that I am not a good SRE. I technically have all the qualifications to be an SRE, which is why in the midst of organizational change I sometimes end up unintentionally in those kinds of roles. But it’s no good for anyone. I sometimes feel guilty about this, but I’m trying to get over this. No one would accidentally assign me to a visual design role, and I would not reluctantly say “sure, I can do that.” But if it’s some kind of programming then I can’t help myself.

But now I feel like I’m back in my wheelhouse. Even though I’m inheriting a project, it was fairly small, within the scope of being completely refactored. I realize I do well when I can keep an entire project in my head, which is a combination of project scope, self-determined architecture, and me investing the time to keep it under control. Nothing about this scales, but that’s why I like early projects. An early project also makes it worth it for me to keep everything in my head, because that gives me the confidence to embrace substantial change and revision as we discover what the project should be.

The specific project I am working on is a voice assistant for the browser. I want to explain why I’m excited about it, but I feel like I have to preface this by acknowledging that my ambitions for the project may never come to pass. I don’t want you, reader, to confuse this for a project roadmap. This is more like a time capsule I’m creating for myself, that I can compare against what we really make.

There’s a few different ways to think about voice interfaces:

  1. Supportive technology for people or situations where other inputs and interfaces don’t work
  2. A way to create more ambient or subtle interfaces, without visual impositions, and without a need for immediate physical presence
  3. A way to interace using natural language instead of defined interfaces with visual feedback establishing the exact options available

As an example of (1) I search for things on my phone using voice because it’s annoying to type some queries. My daughter can use Alexa to play music even though she is not literate. In this area the focus is on algorithms.

A lot of futurism focuses on (3), and it’s kind of the fantasy of ubiquitous household technology. Home voice assistants focus a lot on this, and there’s a lot of focus on hardware.

(3) is what I get excited about. I use voice to create reminders on my phone for this reason, it’s the ability to express something in a sentence that would otherwise require a series of navigations, date inputs, typing input, and perhaps mental calculations.

There’s been experiments in natural-language input in the past, such as Ubiquity. Voice makes that natural language input easier, sloppier, more casual, and deliberately orthogonal to other clicky or typy interfaces. There’s obviously upsides and downsides to these features. But hopefully voice interfaces are a good reason to try again.

In turn there’s two approaches to voice interfaces:

  1. Allow individual applications to define their own voice interaces. Make sure it’s easy for them to get the input they want. Potentially create a hub that will direct voice input to the appropriate application, as a kind of federated approach.
  2. Voice is used to control the singular user agent, that interprets and executes the command. In this model applications at most provide semantically-defined verbs, and the user agent decides how to make use of them.

Traditionally Mozilla has chosen approach (1), often with new Web APIs or specifications. And we may well do so for voice, but in this project we are exploring (2): the user agent collecting and interpreting voice input, and then executing the command as it sees fit, potentially across services, and without the service or application opting-in to anything.

Creating a voice web user agent is both easier and harder than the first approach. It’s harder because interpreting and acting on arbitrary commands, across the extensive diversity of the web, is hard! It’s easier because having done it, the tool actually does something; while having defined a specification, nothing in the world will have changed until and unless people make use of that specification.

Making a voice-based web user agent is, of course, much more work than our little team can possibly do. So we also need a great deal of scaffolding. For this project, desktop Firefox is that scaffolding. The number of things this provides to us is hard to enumerate: installation systems, permissions, account systems, multitasking, interface surface area, and a completely capable fallback to the normal mouse-and-keyboard interface. We’ll be leaning on this very, very heavily for the foreseeable future.

Unfortunately for all its benefits, the ceiling is pretty low on what we can achieve in the context of desktop Firefox. People don’t want to talk to their desktop computers. There’s a good chance you have Siri or Cortana available to you right now. Do you use them? I don’t. Instead there’s this rich, complete, familiar, well-optimized mouse-and-keyboard interface, and I’m at my computer because for whatever I’m doing that’s a good interface.

This sounds pessimistic, and it is, but I don’t want us to create unreasonable expectations. We’re not going to get a lot of people using this tool, at least not in this form factor. If we’re successful, it will be by creating something useful that has lessons and approaches we can translate elsewhere. I have some ideas where that “elsewhere” can be, but for now there’s lots of work and risk in just making something useful.

So what would make this useful?

Visual interfaces involve lots of categorization and narrowing focus: you get things done by navigation, reading categories, parsing visual groupings, until you find a control.

Voice controls just wait for you to say something, then try to make sense of it. There’s no enforced focus or scope, except of course the assistant’s dense incomprehension.

A good assistant will, I think, support commands across services, and without this focus-switch-refocus-act behavior.
