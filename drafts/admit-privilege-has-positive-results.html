We Must Admit To Ourselves That Privilege Has Positive Results

A recent article, The Ethics of Unpaid Labor and the OSS Community, criticizes some employers for using open source contributions as key part of evaluating candidates.

In the interview with Arnold Kling, there was a long discussion about political discourse. One of the things that was particularly striking to me was the observation that pundits primarily argue against believing the “opposition”. That is, liberal pundits (with liberal audiences) primarily argue why you shouldn’t agree with conservatives; conservative pundits argue why you shouldn’t agree with liberals; libertarian pundits argue why both of the other groups are wrong; and so on. They very seldom make positive arguments. And of course who is it better to argue against, the opposition or a strawman?

A few recent posts have reminded me of the discussion of privilege. Today: A few words on privilege… and previously a most peculiar article 20 Things the Rich Do Every Day which seems to serve as a great list for people of different political persuasions to project upon with radically different interpretations. But this applies to just about anything with the word “privilege” in it, a word that has become a shortcut for not exactly a specific interpretation, but a specific kind of statement. Or a more subtle example, The Ethics of Unpaid Labor and the OSS Community.
