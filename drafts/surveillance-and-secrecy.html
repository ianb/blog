Surveillance and Secrecy

I’ve been thinking about surveillance, PRISM, the NSA, a lot recently. As perhaps we all have. And I’ve had a hard time dissecting my own feelings on the matter, identifying exactly what bothers me and what doesn’t.

On the one hand, I’m actually open to the idea of surveillance. How much? I’m not sure. Conducted in what matter? I’m not sure. I don’t know what it should look like, or if it should exist at all.

The concrete outcomes of surveillance aren’t clear at all. A liberal sort of person in the past might have thought “we can’t have surveillance cameras in inconspicuace places, why a negro might appear to look at a white woman in a lustful manner, such that a malicious person with access to the archives could frame any negro man and get him lynched.” But unless you indulge in incredibly hyperbole (which a lot of the people in this discussion do) that wasn’t the outcome. Openness breeds tolerance and liberalism.

So we don’t know what to expect out of changes to surveillance. I for instance think that a surveilled group could be better understood by law enforcement groups, and less likely to have negative stereotypes projected onto them. I don’t have any reason to trust that the police have accurate notions of the cultures and customs of radical groups, so I think there should be a real concern that persecution can proceed from ignorance. But neither group wants to believe this — the police want to believe they are confronting dangerous people, the radicals want to believe they are dangerous to someone enough to expect reprisals, and find conspiratorial explanations of persecution more comforting than the idea it was just a big mistake.

But I don’t know. I’m pretty sure other people don’t know either. How do I know? Ask for the evidence. Not journalistic evidence but real evidence, and the two aren’t very close. The evidence you must as for is what evidence there is that conclusions follow from the provided anecdotes. If you ask “how does the FBI receive political signalling that would make it persecute certain political groups?” and there’s too much handwaving, too many implausible suppositions without enough consideration of less conspiratorial causes. That doesn’t make it impossible that these critiques are right, but it does make it impossible to be certain of outcomes — the best we’re dealing with is political science on the level of alchemy, it is not the stuff of engineering, of predictable results.

What we do have is an iterative political process where we can watch policy effects unfold and adapt the law to stear that effect. I don’t want to get in a truistic gung-ho admiration of democracy, but it does have a number of successes it can point to, some that even the most fervent critic would have to acknoledge so long as they were honest. And you don’t have to believe that the wisdom of democracy comes from the wisdom of the masses to at least see that there is a process of reflection and advocacy and choices spread over a wide set of perspectives that make up the law — even if politics is an insider game, that still includes a far greater set of perspectives than a dictatorship. And even if the effect is not always as direct as we’d like, the wider opinions of society filter their way into these discussions.

But this is all contingent on critical and informed decision making, and when programs are secret that does not happen.
