Desire and Future Worlds

https://medium.com/@ystrickler/resist-and-thrive-1d36819853ca

Reading an article about Le Corbusier in the USSR has made me think about what we build and for whom.

Le Corbusier’s brutalist vision made sense from 10,000 feet; the Radiant City is created for conveniently abstract humans.

http://land8.com/profiles/blogs/skyterra-a-towerable-mistake

But more so I think his designs prioritized comprehensibility for planners. There’s a laziness to it, not just an unwillingness to let the city discover itself, but an unwillingness to even engage with living inhabitants.

This struck me when discussing skyscrapers with a real estate developer.

Minneapolis Iconography No. 4: Skyscrapers by Joe D

We looked out on these buildings, and they seem bland and angular. But they hold real people, every day thousands of hours are experienced in these buildings. The building erases all of this, every floor and window looks the same. These buildings are the well-attired gentleman in a suit who never lets a hint of real emotion escape, whose humanity is trapped beneath a facade of professionalism and authority. What a loss for him and for us, to never know each other.

Though the New Urbanists have entirely different priorities from Corbusier, they also want an environment that is comprehensible, but [comprehensible by the individual]. Their ideal of a street grid is different from the Radiant City, but also very similar, a kind of normalized form in which to build.

We are on the verge of being able to ignore many kinds of comprehensibility. Navigation systems allow us to traverse a city without understanding it. Self-driving cars don’t even need traffic calming or the other rules that we impose via the built structures, as we can trust them to honestly follow policies that we change on a whim.

Commerce can go the same way. Will we need the hierarchy of warehouses, big box stores, local shops, all these categories? Target is a grocery store. Every bookstore must expand to become a coffee shop (or be replaced by those that do adapt). You can pick up your deliveries at Whole Foods. Categories are falling apart, and it seems inevitable that the stores themselves will fade away over time, our desires disintermediated.

The organic city structures we’ll see (organic because what use has nature ever had for comprehensibility?) could go more than one way. It could be like an algae bloom of suburbia, structure without character or meaning. Maybe suburbia is already an expression of this trend. Even in a turn to nostalgia, will these only manifest as microorganisms, a handful of street-facing storefronts that only aspire to be a neighborhood?

The grass grows longest not where it is most healthy, but where it must reach out to get the sunlight it needs. That’s why it’s longest under the picnic table. And we do the same: what we reach for is what we yearn for, the desires we have unfulfilled. We’ll see this too embedded in our future: the city as an expression of unmet desire.
